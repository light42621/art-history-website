Prehistoric (Before 3000 BCE)

Characterized by cave paintings, petroglyphs, and megalithic structures. Art was used for ritualistic or communicative purposes.

Unknown Artists

Anonymous individuals who created early artworks such as the Lascaux cave paintings in France.

The Lascaux cave paintings are over 17,000 years old and depict animals like bulls and deer.

Egyptian (3000–332 BCE)

Focused on afterlife, symbolism, and order. Often stylized and flat, using hierarchical scale.

Thutmose

Thutmose was the royal sculptor of Akhenaten and is known for the famous bust of Queen Nefertiti.

This bust of Queen Nefertiti is renowned for its elegance and was discovered in Amarna.

Greek & Roman (850 BCE – 476 CE)

Focused on idealized human forms, architecture, and mythology. Romans expanded on Greek techniques.

Phidias

Famous Greek sculptor known for his colossal statue of Zeus at Olympia and his work on the Parthenon.

Parthenon sculptures reflect the Greek ideals of balance, proportion, and beauty.

Byzantine (330–1453)

Spiritual, symbolic, and flat in style. Mosaics and icons dominated religious art.

Unknown Artists

Created religious icons and mosaics, such as those found in the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople.

The Deësis mosaic in Hagia Sophia shows Christ flanked by the Virgin Mary and John the Baptist.

Medieval (500–1400)

Art focused on Christian themes and was often symbolic rather than realistic.

Giotto

Early Italian painter known for bringing more naturalism into religious art; considered a forerunner to the Renaissance.

Giotto's "Lamentation" shows emotional depth and foreshortening—hallmarks of proto-Renaissance style.
