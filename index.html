Art Periods

1. Prehistoric 2. Egyptian 3. Greek & Roman 4. Byzantine 5. Medieval 6. Renaissance 7. Mannerism 8. Baroque 9. Rococo 10. Neoclassicism

1. Prehistoric (Before 3000 BCE)

Characterized by cave paintings, petroglyphs, and megalithic structures. Art was used for ritualistic or communicative purposes.

  • Unknown Artists – The creators of the Lascaux cave paintings in France.

2. Egyptian (3000–332 BCE)

Focused on afterlife, symbolism, and order. Often stylized and flat, using hierarchical scale.

  • Thutmose – Credited with the famous bust of Nefertiti.

3. Greek & Roman (850 BCE – 476 CE)

Focused on idealized human forms, architecture, and mythology. Romans expanded on Greek techniques.

  • Phidias – Designed the statue of Zeus at Olympia.

4. Byzantine (330–1453)

Spiritual, symbolic, and flat in style. Mosaics and icons dominated religious art.

  • Unknown Artists – Hagia Sophia mosaics in Constantinople.

5. Medieval (500–1400)

Art focused on Christian themes and was often symbolic rather than realistic.

  • Giotto – Considered the precursor to the Renaissance with his more natural figures.

6. Renaissance (1300–1600)

The Renaissance marked the rebirth of classical art, literature, and humanism. Artists focused on realism, perspective, and human anatomy.

  • Leonardo da Vinci – Known for "Mona Lisa" and "The Last Supper".
  • Michelangelo – Master sculptor and painter of the Sistine Chapel ceiling.

7. Mannerism (1520–1600)

Mannerism reacted to the balance of Renaissance art by introducing elongated forms, exaggerated poses, and tension.

  • El Greco – Known for spiritual intensity and dramatic lighting.

8. Baroque (1600–1750)

Baroque art used motion, drama, and emotional intensity to convey power and religious fervor.

  • Caravaggio – Master of chiaroscuro; known for "The Calling of Saint Matthew".
  • Peter Paul Rubens – Famous for dynamic compositions and sensual figures.

9. Rococo (1720–1780)

Rococo embraced lightness, elegance, and playful themes, often depicting aristocratic leisure in pastel palettes.

  • Jean-Honoré Fragonard – Known for "The Swing", a symbol of Rococo playfulness.

10. Neoclassicism (1750–1830)

Neoclassical art returned to classical ideals of order, simplicity, and heroism, influenced by Enlightenment values.

  • Jacques-Louis David – Known for "Oath of the Horatii" and "Death of Marat".
